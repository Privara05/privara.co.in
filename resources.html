Key DPDPA Concepts

What the Act actually requires — operationally

The DPDPA creates specific operational obligations, not just documentation requirements. These are the concepts most organisations misunderstand.

01
Valid Consent

Consent under the Act must be free, specific, informed, and unambiguous — obtained through a notice that is separate from your terms of service and specific to the purpose of processing. Pre-ticked checkboxes, bundled consent, and consent obtained via terms acceptance do not meet this standard. Consent must also be withdrawable as easily as it was given.

Section 6 — DPDPA Act 2023
02
Privacy Notice Requirements

The Act specifies what your privacy notice must contain — including the personal data being collected, the purpose of processing, how data principal rights can be exercised, and the grievance process. A notice that describes intended practices without being specific to actual system behaviour does not comply. GDPR-adapted notices frequently fail this test.

Section 5 — DPDPA Act 2023
03
Data Processor Obligations

Every third-party tool or service that processes personal data on your behalf must do so under a written contract that specifically governs how personal data is handled. Standard commercial agreements — including most SaaS terms of service — do not meet this requirement. Analytics platforms, CRM tools, email services, and cloud providers all typically fall into this category.

Section 8(3) — DPDPA Act 2023
04
Data Principal Rights

The Act gives individuals the right to access, correct, and erase their personal data — and organisations must have operational processes to fulfil these rights within the Act's prescribed timelines. Account deactivation is not the same as data erasure. A "contact us" email is not the same as an accessible grievance mechanism.

Sections 11–13 — DPDPA Act 2023
05
Purpose Limitation

Personal data may only be processed for the specific purpose disclosed at the point of collection. Using data for secondary purposes — such as analytics, product improvement, or marketing — that are not clearly disclosed in the notice violates the Act's purpose limitation requirement. Each purpose must be specified separately, with a separate consent mechanism where required.

Section 6(1) — DPDPA Act 2023
06
Breach Notification

Organisations must notify the Data Protection Board and affected data principals in the event of a personal data breach. This requires a documented breach response process — not just a policy statement. The Act does not permit notification to be conditional on internal investigation completing first.

Section 8(6) — DPDPA Act 2023
Penalty Reference

DPDPA penalty schedule at a glance

Penalties under the Act are per-violation — not annual caps. Determined by the Data Protection Board. The figures below are the statutory maximums.

Violation Act Reference Maximum Penalty
Failure to implement reasonable security safeguards resulting in a personal data breach Section 8(5) ₹250 crore
Failure to notify the Data Protection Board or affected data principals of a breach Section 8(6) ₹200 crore
Breach of obligations related to processing children's personal data Section 9 ₹200 crore
Failure to fulfil data principal rights — access, correction, erasure, grievance Sections 11–13 ₹50 crore
Non-compliance with any provision of the Act not otherwise specified Section 33 ₹50 crore
Breach of voluntary undertaking given to the Data Protection Board Section 33 ₹10 crore

Penalties are imposed per violation by the Data Protection Board following an inquiry process. The figures above are statutory maximums — actual penalties may be lower based on the Board's assessment. This table is a reference summary and does not constitute legal advice.
